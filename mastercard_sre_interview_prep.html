Day 1 — Foundation
Deepen SRE concepts & sharpen your stories
Focus: SRE theory, observability depth, incident frameworks
Morning (2–3 hrs) — SRE core concepts
SLOs · error budgets · toil · reliability hierarchy
  • Study the SRE triangle: SLIs (what you measure), SLOs (what you commit to), SLAs (contractual). Be ready to define each and give an example from your systems at Informatica or Zurich.
  • Understand error budgets: "If my SLO is 99.9% uptime, I have 8.7 hrs/year of error budget. Here's how I'd manage burn rate." Practice saying this out loud.
  • Toil elimination: review your 75% manual intervention reduction at AVR Edge and your Jenkins/Harness automation work. Frame these as "reducing operational toil so engineers focus on reliability work."
  • Read the Google SRE chapter on "Eliminating Toil" (free online, ~20 mins). Note 2–3 frameworks to reference naturally.
  • MTTD vs MTTR vs MTBF: be precise about which metric your work at Informatica improved and by how much.
Your angle: Your 50% deployment time reduction at Informatica is a strong MTTR story. Reframe it: "We reduced MTTR by shortening deployment cycles and increasing deployment confidence through Harness pipelines."
Late morning (1.5 hrs) — Observability depth
Your strongest technical area — go deep
  • You have a genuine edge here: OpenTelemetry + Honeycomb + ELK at Zurich, plus ELK at Informatica. Prepare a 3-minute architecture walkthrough of your current OTel setup.
  • Know the three pillars: logs, metrics, traces — and crucially, when each is the right signal. The JD asks about "signal quality" specifically.
  • Prepare an answer to: "How do you distinguish noise from signal in your alerts? How do you avoid alert fatigue?" — use your Kibana dashboards experience.
  • Understand cardinality in observability (Honeycomb's key strength) — why high-cardinality data helps debug production incidents faster.
  • Think about a real incident where observability data helped you find the root cause. Structure it as a short story you can tell in 90 seconds.
JD keyword alert: The role says "surfacing early indicators of degradation, not just failure states." Your OTel work at Zurich is perfect evidence — prepare 2 sentences on how your traces/metrics detected issues before users felt them.
Afternoon (2 hrs) — Incident response mastery
Root cause analysis · post-mortems · blameless culture
  • Revisit your Log4j zero-day response (Think Customer First Award). Structure it as: what was the signal → how you investigated → cross-team coordination → resolution → what changed permanently as a result.
  • Study the "5 Whys" and fishbone diagram methods for root cause analysis. The JD explicitly mentions "data-driven investigation techniques."
  • Blameless post-mortems: understand the philosophy and how to run one. Mastercard at this scale will care deeply about post-mortem culture.
  • Prepare an example of an incident that led to a systemic improvement — "not just what broke, but why the system allowed it to break."
  • Review your EBF (Emergency Bug Fix) experience at Informatica — how did you manage post-release incidents and source code merges under pressure?
Key phrase to use: "We treat incidents as learning opportunities, not failures to punish." This language maps directly to what the JD describes.
Evening (1 hr) — Leadership & influence prep
Cross-functional influence without authority
  • This is a Lead role — the SRE manager wants evidence you can influence app, platform, and infra teams. Prepare examples of when you drove reliability decisions without being the manager.
  • Use your mentoring examples (Act As One Team award) to show cross-team leadership.
  • Think of a time you pushed back on a design decision for reliability reasons and won. Or a time you had to compromise — and what you got in return.
  • The JD says "represent the reliability perspective early in the development lifecycle." Prepare a story about shifting left — catching reliability risks before deployment.

Mock answers, Mastercard research & interview readiness

Late morning (2 hrs) — Technical scenario practice
Hands-on problem solving the SRE manager will probe
  • Scenario 1 — Runbook design: "A payment processing service starts showing elevated latency. Walk me through your investigation." Practice: check metrics → traces → logs → narrow to service → check recent deploys → escalate or resolve. Say it out loud.
  • Scenario 2 — SLO definition: "How would you define SLOs for a new microservice?" Your answer: start with user journey, define SLIs (latency p99, error rate, availability), set SLO targets, calculate error budget, set up burn rate alerts.
  • Scenario 3 — Capacity planning: "How do you ensure a service handles 10x traffic?" Cover: load testing, horizontal scaling in K8s (HPA), resource quotas, circuit breakers. You have K8s depth — use it.
  • Scenario 4 — Reducing toil: "What operational work is wasting your team's time and how would you eliminate it?" Use your automation framework (Aspire For Future Award) as the example answer.
  • Review your Kubernetes architecture work at Zurich — be ready to whiteboard or describe it in detail: how OTel collectors are deployed, how traces flow, how you scale the observability platform.
Afternoon (1.5 hrs) — Mock interview & story rehearsal
Say your answers out loud — not just in your head
  • Record yourself answering 3 questions on your phone. Play it back. Are you concise? Do you say "um" excessively? Is your story under 3 minutes?
  • Practice the "tell me about yourself" answer for an SRE manager: lead with your observability and reliability work, not your DevOps/CI-CD background.
  • Rehearse transitions between technical depth and business impact: "This reduced our MTTD by X, which meant fewer customer-facing incidents and less on-call escalation."
  • Prepare 4–5 smart questions to ask the manager. Examples: "What does a typical major incident look like here and how does your team learn from it?" / "How do you balance reliability work with feature delivery pressure?"

Technical — expect these
How do you define and enforce SLOs for a service you own?
Start with user journey → define SLIs (error rate, latency p99, availability) → set SLO target → calculate error budget → configure burn rate alerts in your monitoring stack. Reference your OTel/Honeycomb setup.
Walk me through a production incident you led. What was your process?
Use the Log4j zero-day story. Signal detection → severity assessment → cross-team coordination → structured investigation → resolution → blameless post-mortem → systemic hardening. Emphasise the "why the system allowed it" angle.
How do you identify and eliminate operational toil?
Define toil (manual, repetitive, automatable, scales linearly with load). Reference your 75% manual reduction at AVR Edge and the automation framework you built (Aspire Award). Mention your Jenkins/Harness pipeline work as systematic toil elimination.
Describe your observability architecture. How do you ensure signal quality?
Lead with your Zurich OTel design: collectors, pipelines, Honeycomb for high-cardinality traces, ELK for logs. Explain how you reduced alert noise, built dashboards that show degradation before failure, and how you chose which metrics matter.
How do you approach capacity planning and proactive reliability?
Cover: traffic analysis, load testing (k6/Locust/JMeter), Kubernetes HPA and VPA, resource quotas, historical incident patterns. The JD emphasises "anticipation over reaction" — frame your answer around identifying failure patterns before they cause outages.
Behavioural — expect these
Tell me about a time you influenced a reliability decision without being the manager.
Use your cross-team mentoring examples or a time you pushed back on a deployment decision for reliability reasons. Frame it around data: "I showed the team the error rate data, and we agreed to defer the release."
How do you balance reliability work with feature delivery pressure?
Use error budget as the negotiation tool: "When the error budget is healthy, teams can move fast. When it's burning, we pause feature work to focus on reliability. This removes the argument — the data decides." Show you understand the SRE value proposition.
Describe a time you turned an incident into a lasting improvement.
Log4j response → led to better dependency scanning, Black Duck integration, automated vulnerability alerting. Or your EBF post-release activities that improved source code merge processes. Focus on the systemic change, not just the fix.
Why SRE, and why this role specifically?
Your work IS SRE — you've been doing reliability engineering under a DevOps title. Connect: Mastercard's scale (billions of transactions) demands the kind of proactive, data-driven reliability work you're already doing at Zurich. This role lets you formalise and lead that at greater scale.
